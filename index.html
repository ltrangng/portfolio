I was a low-tech tree hugger.

I love the land and want to see great things growing from it. That’s why I became a landscape architect.

I grew up in Vietnam without the internet. There was no access to computers. There was even scanty food or medicines. Vietnamese who were born before the border was open in 1986 are often seen smaller. That’s how I could see things that big people often miss.

I wanted to see the world, so I traveled. My first trips were on my scooter with a map book along Vietnam. That was before Google and Facebook. Later I couchsurfed in 25 countries with just my smartphone. I like talking with people and learning about their life, their culture, asking about their dreams. That's when I started seeing the limitation of my work. Landscape projects are tied to a physical place and can only be accessed by the people in the region.

The world has changed greatly since the day I hopped on my scooter. How much I love the simplicity of an analog life, I see the endless possibilities of reaching out to people using digital tools. My work has shifted from the built environment to its virtual infrastructure.

What hasn't changed is my interests in different communities, my human-centric design philosophy, and my code of ethics. As a UX Designer, I stand on the frontline of defending the users.
